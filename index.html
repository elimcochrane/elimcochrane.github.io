About me

I am an undergraduate researcher at the University of Kansas pursuing a B.A. in Psychology & Political Science and a B.S. in Data Science (Expected May 2027). My research sits at the intersection of computational linguistics, group identity, and sociopolitical cognition, with a particular focus on online political communities and radicalization.

I am a principal student investigator in the LEMMA Lab under Dr. Mark Landau, where I lead studies combining experimental survey methods and natural language processing techniques to explore how conscious and implicit psychological states differentially impact political behavior. I am applying for PhD programs in social psychology in the upcoming cycle to contribute to scholarship on these issues and expand researchers' capability to design interventions for political extremism.

Theoretical Interests

  • Group Identity & Social Cognition

    Investigating how group-based identity processes, intergroup threat, and collective narratives shape political behavior and discourse.

  • Existential Meaning Construction

    Exploring how people construct and defend meaning through language, belief systems, and group belonging — especially under conditions of uncertainty and identity threat.

  • Political Psychology & Radicalization

    Examining polarization, conspiratorial cognition, and identity construction in politically extreme online communities.

Methodological Interests

  • Psycholinguistics & Natural Language

    Using LIWC-22, BERTopic, VADER, and latent profile analysis to study language patterns in large-scale social media corpora.

  • Machine Learning & Text Classification

    Applying transformer-based models, dimensionality reduction, and semantic similarity methods to classify and interpret large corpora of political and social text.

  • Experimental & Survey Design

    Designing and deploying controlled experiments and Qualtrics surveys to test causal claims about identity, motivation, and political cognition.
